  • Stuck? Even length (e.g. cbbd) needs a center between two chars.

The punchline: a palindrome is a center core

Don’t open with “try every pair of endpoints.” Once you name the center, the longest palindrome around it is unique: keep expanding while the two sides agree. So we enumerate centers, not substrings.

One cheat sheet

Two kinds of centers (about \(2n-1\) of them)

\[ \text{odd length: center on char }i \quad(L,R)\leftarrow(i,i) \] \[ \text{even length: center in the gap }i\,|\,i{+}1 \quad(L,R)\leftarrow(i,i+1) \]

Expand while the mirrors match

\[ \text{while } L\ge 0 \land R<n \land s[L]=s[R]: \quad L\leftarrow L-1,\; R\leftarrow R+1 \]

When the loop stops, \(L\) and \(R\) overshot — the real span is

\[ [L+1,\, R-1] \] Track the global best \((\mathrm{start},\,\mathrm{len})\).
Not problem 3. Problem 3’s sliding window chases “no duplicate chars.” Here the constraint is left–right symmetry — expand around centers, not last-index windows.

Where this shows up why care

  • Spotting mirrored phrases in text / DNA teaching demos.
  • Catching symmetric patterns in IDs or toy password generators.
  • String algorithms 101: expand is the intuition under Manacher.
  • Contrast with longest palindromic subsequence — skip allowed → different model.

Expand around centers is not fancy for fancy’s sake. Palindrome means symmetry. Parameterize by the axis, and “all substrings” collapses to “all axes” — constant space, short code.

Approaches · pick your weapon compare

A. Brute force
Time \(O(n^3)\) Space \(O(1)\)

Every substring, then a linear palindrome check. Crystal clear; \(n=1000\) is already sweaty.

B. Expand around centers (recommended)
Time \(O(n^2)\) Space \(O(1)\)

~\(2n-1\) centers, expand each, keep the longest span. What you buy: you stop enumerating endpoint pairs — you only walk symmetry axes.

C. Interval DP
Time \(O(n^2)\) Space \(O(n^2)\)

\(\mathrm{dp}[i][j]=(s[i]=s[j])\land(\text{short or }dp[i+1][j-1])\). Correct, heavier. Expand is closer to the definition.

Why two center types? gotcha

If you only expand from a character, you miss even-length pals like bb and abba — their mirror axis sits in the gap between two characters.

TypeSeedPicture
Odd center \(L=R=i\) aba — axis on b
Even center \(L=i,\;R=i+1\) abba — axis between the two bs
One helper does both: expand(l, r). Odd → expand(i, i). Even → expand(i, i+1). Same loop, two seeds. No drama.

Ex 2 · cbbd (even is mandatory)

Character centers top out at length 1. Seed the gap between the two bs: \(L=1, R=2\) → bb. That’s the answer.

Off-by-one after expand

The while loop dies when it hits a mismatch or the border — so \(L\) and \(R\) have already stepped one too far. The true palindrome is \([L+1, R-1]\), length \(R-L-1\).

What if the rules change? riffs

  • Only need the length → same expand, return \(\mathrm{len}\) only.
  • Longest palindromic subsequence → skips allowed → DP territory.
  • \(n\) up to \(10^5\) and strict linear → Manacher.
  • Count all palindromes → sum the radius from each center.
